Exploring the Cultures of Ghana

A journey through the vibrant traditions and heritage of Ghana’s major ethnic groups

Discover Now

Welcome to Cultures of Ghana, your gateway to understanding the rich tapestry of Ghanaian heritage. Created by a passionate team of cultural enthusiasts, this site aims to educate and celebrate the diverse ethnic groups that shape Ghana’s identity.

We envision a world where Ghana’s cultural diversity is cherished and understood globally. Our mission is to preserve and share the traditions, languages, and histories of Ghana’s people.

Akan

The Akan, Ghana’s largest ethnic group (49.1% of the population), dominate southern and central Ghana, including Ashanti, Central, Eastern, Western, Bono, Ahafo, and parts of Volta regions, extending into southeastern Ivory Coast.

The Akan are organized into eight matrilineal clans (abusua), each with a totem:

  • Oyoko (Falcon): Patience, bravery; Kumasi, Dwaben.
  • Bretuo (Leopard): Aggressiveness; Mampong, Amoafo.
  • Aduana (Dog): Hardworking; Dormaa, Kumawu.
  • Asona (Crow): Wisdom; Edweso, Offinso.
  • Agona (Parrot): Eloquence; Nkawie, Denkyira.
  • Ekuona (Buffalo): Uprightness; Fomena.
  • Asenie (Bat): Diplomacy; Amakom, Dompoase.
  • Asakyiri (Vulture): Beauty; Akorokyere.

The Akan follow matrilineal descent: inheritance (land, titles) passes through the mother’s line to nephews or brothers, not sons. Fathers contribute the “spirit” (ntoro), while mothers provide “blood” (mogya) and clan identity.

Marriage is exogamous (outside the clan), requiring a small gift (e.g schnapps) to the bride’s family. Children join the mother’s clan. Polygyny was common historically, with queen mothers overseeing disputes.

The Adaduanan calendar has nine 42-day cycles. Key days are Akwasidae (Sundays, public rites) and Awukudae (Wednesdays, ancestral offerings), now aligned with the Gregorian calendar.

  • Adae: Every 42 days; honors ancestors with drumming and libations.
  • Odwira: Annual purification (Sept/Oct); processions in Akyem, Asante.
  • Apoo: Satirical cleansing in western Akan areas (e.g., Nzema).
  • Kundum: Harvest festival with dance (Ahanta, Nzema).

Key towns include Kumasi, Mampong, Offinso (Ashanti); Cape Coast, Elmina (Central); Akropong, Kyebi (Eastern); Nkawie, Axim (Western); Dormaa, Techiman (Ahafo).

Ga-Adangbe

The Ga-Adangbe people, primarily in Greater Accra, are known for their vibrant Homowo festival and Kpanlogo drumming. They make up about 7.4% of Ghana’s population.

  • Homowo: Celebrates harvest with yam dishes and sprinkling kpokpoi (May-June).
  • Kpledomi: Religious festival with traditional dances.

Key towns include Accra, Tema, Osu, and La.

Guans

Considered one of the earliest inhabitants of Ghana, the Guans are scattered across central and southern regions. They have a rich oral tradition and are known for their farming and fishing practices.

The Guans are skilled farmers and fishermen, with festivals like Ohum celebrating yam harvests.

Ewe

The Ewe people, primarily in the Volta Region, are renowned for their intricate weaving (like Ewe Kente) and vibrant festivals such as Hogbetsotso, which commemorates their migration history.

Hogbetsotso: Commemorates Ewe migration with dances and storytelling (November).

Mole-Dagbani

Found in northern Ghana, the Mole-Dagbani are known for their historic kingdoms like Dagbon. They excel in architecture (mud-and-stick mosques) and celebrate festivals like Damba with music and dance.

Damba: Celebrates the Prophet Muhammad’s birth with music and horse parades.
